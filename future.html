Does This Sound Familiar?

Your household runs on a good income. Your financial plan still runs on defaults.

Your W-4 may be untouched since your first day on the job. The 401(k) gets its automatic percentage. Savings sit where they landed. Then your entire tax strategy gets squeezed into one rushed CPA meeting in April.

You can be careful with money and still feel this pressure when one of the largest bills in your house gets paid without a plan built around your income, accounts, and the future you want for your kids.

Your CPA may be doing exactly what you hired them to do: filing an accurate return. But an accurate return can still document an overpayment if no one planned during the year.

Design the Year in Advance

Your family can't redesign a tax year after it closes.

The families who keep more don't wait for filing season. They plan the year around their income, accounts, investments, and the people depending on them.

Some tax moves must be completed by December 31st. Others have earlier deadlines. By April, your income has been earned, the money has been spent, and the tax has already been withheld. Your CPA can only report what happened.

Starting in July gives you time to ask better questions, set up the right accounts and structures, and complete the paperwork before those options disappear.

About Me

If we haven't met, my name's Preston.

8-Figure Investor, Legacy Investing Show

I grew up in a first-generation immigrant family. My parents worked constantly and saved what they could, but nobody ever sat us down and explained how money really works in this country.

I got good grades and found a good job, just like you're supposed to. Then I landed in software sales and hated almost every day of it. The paychecks grew, the tax bill grew faster, and the life I wanted kept getting pushed back another year.

So I studied the tax code and the investors who use it well. Over time, I built a $15M+ real estate portfolio from scratch. By 28, my wife and I had both quit our jobs. One year, I made $500K and got a $46 federal refund. That was my result. Yours will depend on your own tax situation.

Since then I've reviewed over a thousand tax returns from high-earning households. The biggest gaps usually came from decisions that were never made during the year, not math errors on the return.

Today we have two little boys. My wife and I run our household on this system because keeping more only matters if the plan also protects the people and future we're building it for. This workshop is the plain-English version of what I wish someone had handed my parents 30 years ago.
